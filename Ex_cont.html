SPICES

For generations, Sri Lanka has been renowned for its wonderful variety of spices. These spices are significantly responsible for the delectable flavors of Sri Lankan food, including curries made with vegetables, red or white meat, fish, seafood, or even some sweetmeat. The Sri Lankan spice and concentrate market today offers a wide range of goods, from bulk spices like real cinnamon to essential oils with added value. Smallholders predominate in the sector, and smallholdings and household gardens account for almost 70% of all cultivated land. Every year, Sri Lanka exports over 30,000 tons of diverse spices. Ceylon cinnamon, Ceylon pepper, Ceylon cloves, Ceylon cardamom, Ceylon nutmeg, mace, and vanilla are the primary spices from Sri Lanka. A variety of chilies, coriander seeds, leaves, curry leaves, cumin, fennel, fenugreek, ginger, mustard, tamarind, and turmeric are among the herbs and additives that can be found in Sri Lanka. Sri Lanka exports 56% of its other agricultural commodities, including spices and associated goods and essential oils, while 55% of its exports are cinnamon and related goods. Ceylon spices provide many purposes than only flavoring. Most of them have significant medicinal characteristics and are frequently utilized in pharmacology and complementary medicine. Because of their distinct scent, some of them, like genuine cinnamon and cloves, are used as ingredients in the perfume and cosmetic industries. Moreover, a variety of seasonings and condiments that give flavor to all Sri Lankan foods, including from rice to curries, pickles, salads, sambols, and sweets, are made from spices and herbs from Sri Lanka that have been mixed and processed into powder or paste. Moreover, a variety of essential oils, including cinnamon leaf oil, clove oil, cinnamon bark oil, nutmeg oil, pepper oil, citronella oil, sesame oil, and cardamom oil, are extracted from Sri Lankan spices. Cinnamon oil tops the list of the many essential oils produced in Sri Lanka due to its multiple advantages that have been known to mankind for ages. The cinnamon leaf oil, which is obtained by steam distilling the leaves of the cinnamon tree, has phenols, eugenol, and small amounts of cinnamon aldehyde, a flavoring ingredient and a potent insect and mosquito repellant. Moreover, cinnamon bark oil, which is more expensive, has potent antibacterial qualities. Oleoresins, which are blends of volatile and non-volatile botanicals derived from Sri Lankan spices such as black pepper, cardamom, nutmeg, ginger, cloves, garcinia, lemongrass, and cinnamon, are another product that Sri Lanka produces and exports. Sri Lanka provides essential oils, oleoresins, and spices that are both conventional and organic. The number of alkaloids and flavonoids that give each spice its flavor, pungency, and scent are largely influenced by the weather and soil. Due to their distinctive flavor and perfume, Sri Lankan spices are known by the distinctive geographical indication of Ceylon Spices, which has helped the country gain international recognition since the 14th century. >
